Introduction: ClassifyingMarine Mammal Diving Behavior

›

Marine mammals such as whales and dolphins have evolved remarkable diving capabilities that allow them to explore the ocean depths in search of food, mates, and shelter. Understanding these diving behaviors is crucial for marine conservation efforts and provides insights into how these animals have adapted to their environment.

This analysis examines high-resolution bio-logging data collected from tagged marine mammals, specifically focusing on dive profiles, movement patterns, and behavioral states during dives. By analyzing the depth, duration, and movement intensity of dives, the research aims to identify different behavioral states and potentially infer foraging and other important activities.

Data Source: The data was collected using animal-borne tags attached to marine mammals. These tags record depth, acceleration, temperature, and other parameters at high sampling rates (typically 5-10 Hz), providing detailed information about the animals' movements and environmental conditions they experience.

The quest is to answer the following questions:

  • What are the typical diving patterns exhibited by these animals?
  • Can distinct phases within diving cycles be identified?
  • How does movement intensity vary across different dive phases and depths?
  • Are there relationships between dive duration and depth that might indicate different behavioral states?

Dive Depth Distribution: The Foundation of Diving Behavior

Understanding the distribution of dive depths provides critical insight into habitat use and potential foraging zones. The histogram below shows the frequency distribution of dive depths recorded by the tag.

Figure 1: Distribution of dive depths showing distinct patterns at different depth ranges. Three key zones are identified: surface (0-5m), shallow (5-15m), and deep (>15m) dives.

The depth distribution reveals several interesting patterns:

  • Surface periods (0-5m) represent time when the animal is breathing and recovering between dives
  • Shallow dives (5-15m) may represent exploratory behavior or transitions between surface and deep dives
  • Deep dives (>15m) likely represent focused foraging efforts, where the animal targets specific prey layers

The multimodal distribution suggests different functional categories of dives, each potentially serving a different purpose. The thresholds at 5m and 15m (indicated by red dashed lines) represent biologically meaningful transitions between these diving states.

Cluster Analysis of Dive Cycles: Identifying Behavioral Patterns

To identify natural groupings in diving behavior without imposing arbitrary thresholds, a cluster analysis was performed based on depth and time parameters. This unsupervised machine learning approach helps reveal inherent patterns in diving behavior.

Figure 2: K-means cluster analysis identifying three distinct dive behavior clusters. Colors represent different clusters identified by the algorithm.

The cluster analysis reveals three distinct behavioral states:

  • Cluster 1: Deep dives - characterized by longer durations and greater depths, likely representing dedicated foraging events
  • Cluster 2: Intermediate dives - moderate depths and durations, possibly representing exploratory or transitional behavior
  • Cluster 3: Surface periods - minimal depth, representing breathing and recovery periods between dives

The emergence of these clusters supports the hypothesis that diving behavior is not continuous but can be categorized into discrete functional states. This clustering approach provides an objective method for classifying diving behavior without relying solely on predefined depth thresholds.

Relationship Between Dive Duration and Depth

The relationship between how long an animal dives and how deep it goes can reveal physiological constraints and behavioral strategies. This relationship helps understand the trade-offs these animals make when diving.

Figure 3: Relationship between dive duration and depth, color-coded by dive phase. The trend line indicates the general relationship between these variables.

This visualization reveals several key insights:

  • A positive correlation between dive duration and depth - deeper dives generally take longer
  • Different dive phases (colors) occupy different regions of the depth-duration space
  • Significant variability around the trend line suggests that factors beyond depth influence dive duration

This relationship aligns with diving theory: deeper dives require more travel time and may offer access to different prey resources, justifying the longer duration. However, physiological constraints like oxygen storage capacity limit how long animals can remain at depth.

Temporal Distribution of Dive Phases

Understanding how diving behavior changes over time can reveal activity patterns, responses to environmental conditions, and behavioral sequences. The plot below shows how different dive phases are distributed over time.

Figure 4: Temporal sequence of dive phases throughout the recording period. This visualization reveals how the animal transitions between different behavioral states over time.

The temporal distribution reveals several patterns:

  • Cyclic patterns of deep dives followed by recovery at the surface
  • Periods of concentrated deep diving activity that may indicate foraging bouts
  • Transitions between phases that suggest a structured diving strategy rather than random behavior

By examining these temporal patterns, it's possible to identify behavioral sequences that might be linked to specific ecological functions like foraging, traveling, or resting. The regularity of certain sequences suggests deliberate behavioral strategies rather than opportunistic responses.

Movement Intensity Across Dive Phases: Understanding Energy Expenditure

Movement intensity, measured by the normalized jerk (rate of change of acceleration), provides a proxy for energy expenditure and activity level during different dive phases. This metric helps identify active foraging or prey capture events.

Figure 5: Movement intensity (normalized jerk) across different dive phases and depths. Higher values indicate more intense movement that may be associated with foraging or prey capture attempts.

This faceted plot reveals distinct movement patterns across dive phases:

  • Deep Phase: Shows bursts of high-intensity movement at specific depths, likely indicating foraging events
  • Shallow Phase: Generally less intense movement, possibly representing exploratory behavior
  • Surface Phase: Relatively low movement intensity, consistent with recovery behavior
  • Surfacing: Brief increases in movement associated with the act of surfacing and breathing

The relationship between depth and movement intensity within each phase provides evidence for depth-specific foraging strategies and helps identify the depths where prey resources might be concentrated.

These comprehensive visualizations allow examination of:

  • Peaks in movement intensity that may represent prey capture attempts
  • Relationships between depth, temperature, and behavioral parameters
  • How specific events fit within the broader context of dive sequences

By integrating these multiple data streams, a more complete picture can be built of what the animal is doing throughout different phases of a dive, revealing complex behavioral sequences that wouldn't be apparent from depth data alone.

Conclusions and Future ML-Driven Research

The analysis has revealed several key insights about marine mammal diving behavior that can inform the development of machine learning workflows:

  • Diving behavior naturally clusters into distinct functional states that can serve as target classes for supervised learning
  • The relationship between dive parameters (depth, duration, movement intensity) provides feature-rich data for classification algorithms
  • Temporal patterns in dive sequences could be leveraged for time-series prediction models
  • Movement intensity signatures show promise as discriminative features for identifying specific behavioral events

Optimizing Dataset for ML Classification:

  • Class balancing techniques will be necessary to address the uneven distribution of dive phases (surface events being more frequent than deep dives)
  • Feature engineering should emphasize the most discriminative parameters identified in the analysis
  • Temporal context windows can capture the sequential nature of diving behavior
  • Data augmentation techniques may help address the limited number of tagged animals

Human-in-the-Loop Annotation Refinement:

  • A clustering-based annotation suggestion system will be implemented that proposes groupings of similar behaviors
  • An interactive visualization UI will allow human experts to validate or modify these suggested groupings
  • This iterative refinement process will improve label quality while reducing annotation time
  • Uncertainty metrics will highlight ambiguous classifications that require expert review

ML Pipeline Development:

  • Preprocessing will normalize and segment the continuous bio-logging data into analysis-ready formats
  • Feature selection will be guided by the domain insights gained from the exploratory analysis
  • Model selection will explore both traditional classifiers and deep learning approaches for time-series classification
  • Evaluation metrics will focus on correctly identifying behaviorally significant events, with particular emphasis on rare but important behaviors

3D Data-Driven Simulation with NVIDIA NVAIE & NIMs:

Future work will include developing a data-driven 3D simulation pipeline that brings the behavioral analyses to life using NVIDIA's AI and simulation technologies:

  • Kinematic-Driven Animation: The high-resolution accelerometer and orientation data from the tags will drive a rigged 3D model of a whale, translating the raw kinematic measurements into realistic motion in real-time
  • NVIDIA Omniverse AI Explorer (NVAIE): Will provide the real-time rendering environment where the whale's movements can be visualized in a physically accurate underwater setting
  • Neural Implicit Models (NIMs): Can be used to generate smooth, continuous representations of movement patterns from discrete sensor data, improving animation fluidity
  • Behavioral State Visualization: The ML-classified behavioral states will be color-coded or otherwise visually indicated in the 3D environment, creating an intuitive representation of the animal's changing behavioral states

Integration with Annotation Workflow:

  • The 3D visualization will serve as a validation tool for annotations, allowing experts to visually confirm if classified behaviors match expected movement patterns
  • Ambiguous classifications can be reviewed in the 3D environment, providing additional context for human decision-making
  • Iterative refinement between the ML models and 3D visualization will create a feedback loop that improves both classification accuracy and animation realism

Future Expansions:

  • Integration of bathymetry data to recreate the actual underwater terrain where the behaviors occurred
  • Addition of GPS tracks to show spatial movement patterns in relation to underwater features
  • Environmental data layers (temperature, salinity, prey density) to contextualize behaviors within oceanographic conditions
  • Multi-animal simulations to visualize social interactions and group behaviors

This data-driven approach to marine mammal behavior classification represents a step toward automating the analysis of bio-logging data. By combining machine learning techniques with domain expertise through human-in-the-loop validation, robust tools can be developed for understanding the complex underwater behavior of these fascinating animals and contribute to their conservation through more efficient data processing and pattern recognition. The bottom line is that accurate classification of feeding versus non-feeding behaviors will allow environmental modeling accuracy to be optimized through rigorous annotation validation and refinement steps. This approach ensures that downstream environmental models can reliably correlate animal behavior with oceanographic features, ultimately leading to more accurate predictions of how marine mammals respond to changing ocean conditions.
